01. Gazebo Physics

Simulating Real-World Dynamics and Sensors​

This chapter explores the use of Gazebo, a powerful 3D robot simulator, for accurately modeling real-world physics and sensor interactions. It covers fundamental concepts of physics engines within simulation, including rigid body dynamics, collision detection, and joint constraints. The chapter will detail how to configure and integrate various sensor models (e.g., cameras, LiDAR, IMUs) within Gazebo to mimic their real-world counterparts, providing realistic data streams for AI development. Emphasis will be placed on understanding the nuances of simulation accuracy, performance optimization, and the critical role of high-fidelity physics in training robust robotic systems.

Introduction to Gazebo​

Gazebo (formerly known as Gazebo Classic, with the newer version called Ignition Gazebo or Gazebo Sim) is an open-source 3D robotics simulator that provides accurate physics simulation, high-quality graphics rendering, and sensor simulation. It's tightly integrated with the Robot Operating System (ROS and ROS 2), making it the de facto standard for robotics research and development.

Why Use Gazebo?​

Advantages:

  • Cost-Effective: Test algorithms without risking expensive hardware
  • Safety: Experiment with potentially dangerous behaviors in simulation
  • Reproducibility: Controlled environments enable repeatable experiments
  • Scalability: Run multiple parallel simulations for training
  • Rapid Iteration: Faster development cycles than physical prototyping
  • ROS Integration: Native support for ROS topics, services, and plugins

Common Use Cases:

  • Algorithm development and testing
  • Robot design validation
  • Multi-robot coordination
  • Reinforcement learning training
  • Hardware-in-the-loop testing
  • Education and demonstrations

Physics Engines​

Gazebo supports multiple physics engines, each with different characteristics and trade-offs.

ODE (Open Dynamics Engine)​

Characteristics:

  • Default physics engine in Gazebo Classic
  • Stable and well-tested
  • Good general-purpose performance
  • Supports various constraint types

Strengths:

  • Mature codebase with extensive use
  • Handles complex contact scenarios
  • Good for rigid body dynamics

Limitations:

  • Can be computationally expensive for complex scenes
  • Less accurate than modern alternatives for certain scenarios

Bullet​

Characteristics:

  • Used extensively in gaming and VFX
  • Fast collision detection
  • Good multi-threading support

Strengths:

  • Excellent performance for large numbers of objects
  • Robust collision handling
  • Well-optimized

Limitations:

  • Sometimes less stable for complex constraints
  • Tuning parameters can be tricky

Simbody​

Characteristics:

  • Focus on biomechanics and accurate dynamics
  • High-quality contact modeling
  • Used in scientific simulations

Strengths:

  • Very accurate for complex kinematic chains
  • Excellent for human/animal locomotion
  • Sophisticated constraint handling

Limitations:

  • Slower than ODE or Bullet
  • Higher computational requirements

DART (Dynamic Animation and Robotics Toolkit)​

Characteristics:

  • Modern physics engine
  • Analytical gradients for optimization
  • Focus on robotics applications

Strengths:

  • Excellent for trajectory optimization
  • Differentiable physics
  • Good performance

Choosing a Physics Engine​

<!-- In world file (.world) -->
<world name="default">
  <physics type="ode">
    <max_step_size>0.001</max_step_size>
    <real_time_factor>1.0</real_time_factor>
    <real_time_update_rate>1000</real_time_update_rate>
  </physics>
  <!-- Or use: type="bullet", "simbody", "dart" -->
</world>

Decision Factors:

  • Speed vs. Accuracy: ODE/Bullet for speed, Simbody for accuracy
  • Contact Complexity: Bullet for many contacts, Simbody for precise contacts
  • Optimization Needs: DART if you need gradients
  • Stability: ODE is most battle-tested

World and Model Description: SDF and URDF​

SDF (Simulation Description Format)​

SDF is Gazebo's native XML format for describing robots, environments, sensors, and physics.

Example World File:

<?xml version="1.0"?>
<sdf version="1.6">
  <world name="robot_world">
    <!-- Physics configuration -->
    <physics type="ode">
      <max_step_size>0.001</max_step_size>
      <real_time_factor>1.0</real_time_factor>
      <gravity>0 0 -9.81</gravity>
    </physics>

    <!-- Lighting -->
    <light name="sun" type="directional">
      <pose>0 0 10 0 0 0</pose>
      <diffuse>1 1 1 1</diffuse>
      <specular>0.2 0.2 0.2 1</specular>
      <direction>0 0 -1</direction>
    </light>

    <!-- Ground plane -->
    <model name="ground_plane">
      <static>true</static>
      <link name="link">
        <collision name="collision">
          <geometry>
            <plane>
              <normal>0 0 1</normal>
              <size>100 100</size>
            </plane>
          </geometry>
          <surface>
            <friction>
              <ode>
                <mu>100</mu>
                <mu2>50</mu2>
              </ode>
            </friction>
          </surface>
        </collision>
        <visual name="visual">
          <geometry>
            <plane>
              <normal>0 0 1</normal>
              <size>100 100</size>
            </plane>
          </geometry>
          <material>
            <ambient>0.8 0.8 0.8 1</ambient>
          </material>
        </visual>
      </link>
    </model>
  </world>
</sdf>

  <!-- <gravity>0 0 -3.71</gravity> --> <!-- Mars gravity -->

Adjusting gravity is useful for:

  • Testing robustness under different planetary conditions
  • Simplifying dynamics for learning (lower gravity = slower falls)
  • Stress-testing control algorithms

Friction and Surface Properties​

Friction is critical for realistic wheel-ground interaction, grasping, and contact dynamics.

Coulomb Friction Model:

<surface>
  <friction>
    <ode>
      <mu>1.0</mu>      <!-- Friction coefficient (direction 1) -->
      <mu2>1.0</mu2>    <!-- Friction coefficient (direction 2) -->
      <slip1>0.0</slip1>
      <slip2>0.0</slip2>
    </ode>
  </friction>
  <contact>
    <ode>
      <kp>1000000.0</kp>  <!-- Contact stiffness -->
      <kd>100.0</kd>      <!-- Contact damping -->
      <max_vel>0.01</max_vel>
      <min_depth>0.001</min_depth>
    </ode>
  </contact>
</surface>

Friction Coefficients:

  • Rubber on concrete: μ ≈ 1.0
  • Metal on metal: μ ≈ 0.15-0.25
  • Ice on ice: μ ≈ 0.02
  • Teflon on Teflon: μ ≈ 0.04

Inertia and Mass Properties​

Accurate inertia is crucial for realistic dynamics.

Computing Inertia:

For simple shapes:

  • Box: Ixx = (m/12)(h² + d²), Iyy = (m/12)(w² + d²), Izz = (m/12)(w² + h²)
  • Cylinder: Ixx = Iyy = (m/12)(3r² + h²), Izz = (m/2)r²
  • Sphere: Ixx = Iyy = Izz = (2/5)mr²

For complex meshes, use CAD software to compute inertia tensors.

<inertial>
  <mass value="5.0"/>
  <origin xyz="0 0 0" rpy="0 0 0"/>
  <inertia ixx="0.083" ixy="0.0" ixz="0.0"
           iyy="0.083" iyz="0.0" izz="0.083"/>
</inertial>

Common Mistakes:

  • Zero or very small mass/inertia (causes instability)
  • Incorrect inertia tensor (non-physical behavior)
  • Center of mass misalignment

Collision Detection​

Collision Geometries:

Use simplified collision meshes for performance:

<collision name="collision">
  <geometry>
    <!-- Simple primitive (fast) -->
    <box size="1 1 1"/>
    <!-- Or mesh (slower but accurate) -->
    <!-- <mesh><uri>model://robot/meshes/collision.stl</uri></mesh> -->
  </geometry>
</collision>

Best Practices:

  • Use primitives (box, sphere, cylinder) when possible
  • Decompose complex shapes into convex hulls
  • Separate visual and collision meshes
  • Avoid deeply nested geometries

Spawning Robots Programmatically​

Python Script:

#!/usr/bin/env python3
import rclpy
from rclpy.node import Node
from gazebo_msgs.srv import SpawnEntity
import os

class RobotSpawner(Node):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__('robot_spawner')
        self.spawn_client = self.create_client(SpawnEntity, '/spawn_entity')

        while not self.spawn_client.wait_for_service(timeout_sec=1.0):
            self.get_logger().info('Waiting for spawn_entity service...')

        self.spawn_robot()

    def spawn_robot(self):
        # Read robot description from file
        urdf_path = os.path.join(
            os.path.expanduser('~'),
            'robot_ws/src/my_robot/urdf/robot.urdf'
        )

        with open(urdf_path, 'r') as f:
            robot_desc = f.read()

        # Create spawn request
        request = SpawnEntity.Request()
        request.name = 'my_robot'
        request.xml = robot_desc
        request.robot_namespace = 'robot1'
        request.initial_pose.position.x = 0.0
        request.initial_pose.position.y = 0.0
        request.initial_pose.position.z = 0.5

        # Call service
        future = self.spawn_client.call_async(request)
        rclpy.spin_until_future_complete(self, future)

        if future.result() is not None:
            self.get_logger().info(f'Successfully spawned {request.name}')
        else:
            self.get_logger().error('Failed to spawn robot')

def main():
    rclpy.init()
    spawner = RobotSpawner()
    rclpy.shutdown()

if __name__ == '__main__':
    main()

Controlling Robot from ROS 2​

#!/usr/bin/env python3
import rclpy
from rclpy.node import Node
from geometry_msgs.msg import Twist

class RobotController(Node):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__('robot_controller')
        self.publisher = self.create_publisher(Twist, '/cmd_vel', 10)
        self.timer = self.create_timer(0.1, self.move_robot)
        self.count = 0

    def move_robot(self):
        msg = Twist()

        # Move in a circle
        msg.linear.x = 0.5  # Forward velocity (m/s)
        msg.angular.z = 0.5  # Angular velocity (rad/s)

        self.publisher.publish(msg)
        self.count += 1

        if self.count > 100:  # Stop after 10 seconds
            msg.linear.x = 0.0
            msg.angular.z = 0.0
            self.publisher.publish(msg)
            self.get_logger().info('Stopping robot')
            raise SystemExit

def main():
    rclpy.init()
    controller = RobotController()
    rclpy.spin(controller)
    rclpy.shutdown()

if __name__ == '__main__':
    main()

Best Practices and Performance Optimization​

Simulation Fidelity vs. Speed​

Trade-offs:

  • Higher physics update rate → More accurate but slower
  • Complex collision meshes → Realistic but computationally expensive
  • Sensor noise models → Better sim-to-real but adds overhead

Recommendations:

<!-- For real-time interactive simulation -->
<physics type="ode">
  <max_step_size>0.001</max_step_size>
  <real_time_update_rate>1000</real_time_update_rate>
  <real_time_factor>1.0</real_time_factor>
</physics>

<!-- For faster-than-real-time training -->
<physics type="bullet">
  <max_step_size>0.01</max_step_size>
  <real_time_update_rate>100</real_time_update_rate>
  <real_time_factor>0</real_time_factor> <!-- Run as fast as possible -->
</physics>

Model Optimization​

1. Simplify Collision Geometry:

<!-- Bad: High-poly mesh -->
<collision>
  <geometry>
    <mesh><uri>detailed_model.dae</uri></mesh>
  </geometry>
</collision>

<!-- Good: Primitive approximation -->
<collision>
  <geometry>
    <box size="1 0.5 0.3"/>
  </geometry>
</collision>

2. Reduce Sensor Update Rates:

  • Cameras: 10-30 Hz (not 60+)
  • LiDAR: 10-20 Hz
  • IMU: 100-200 Hz (not 1000+)

3. Disable Unnecessary Features:

<visual>
  <cast_shadows>false</cast_shadows> <!-- Disable shadows for better performance -->
</visual>

4. Use Level of Detail (LOD):

<visual>
  <geometry>
    <mesh>
      <uri>model://robot/meshes/visual.dae</uri>
    </mesh>
  </geometry>
</visual>

    output='screen'

Debugging Physics Issues​

Enable Visualization:

# View contact points
gzclient --verbose
# In GUI: View → Contacts

Check Physics Parameters:

#!/usr/bin/env python3
import rclpy
from gazebo_msgs.srv import GetModelState

# Query model state for debugging
def check_model_state(model_name):
    rclpy.init()
    node = rclpy.create_node('state_checker')
    client = node.create_client(GetModelState, '/gazebo/get_model_state')

    request = GetModelState.Request()
    request.model_name = model_name

    future = client.call_async(request)
    rclpy.spin_until_future_complete(node, future)

    if future.result():
        state = future.result()
        print(f"Position: {state.pose.position}")
        print(f"Orientation: {state.pose.orientation}")
        print(f"Linear velocity: {state.twist.linear}")
        print(f"Angular velocity: {state.twist.angular}")

Common Issues and Solutions​

Issue 1: Robot Falls Through Ground​

Cause: Collision geometry not properly defined or very small mass Solution:

<!-- Ensure ground has collision -->
<collision name="ground_collision">
  <geometry>
    <plane><normal>0 0 1</normal></plane>
  </geometry>
</collision>

<!-- Ensure robot has reasonable mass -->
<inertial>
  <mass value="1.0"/> <!-- Not 0.001 -->
</inertial>

Issue 2: Unstable Simulation​

Cause: Time step too large or contact parameters incorrect Solution:

<physics>
  <max_step_size>0.001</max_step_size> <!-- Smaller step -->
  <ode>
    <solver>
      <iters>50</iters> <!-- Increase solver iterations -->
    </solver>
    <constraints>
      <cfm>0.0</cfm>
      <erp>0.2</erp>
      <contact_max_correcting_vel>100.0</contact_max_correcting_vel>
      <contact_surface_layer>0.001</contact_surface_layer>
    </constraints>
  </ode>
</physics>

Issue 3: Slow Simulation​

Causes: Too many contacts, complex meshes, high update rates Solutions:

  • Simplify collision meshes
  • Reduce sensor update rates
  • Switch to faster physics engine (Bullet)
  • Disable GUI (headless mode)
  • Reduce real-time factor

Issue 4: Inaccurate Sensor Data​

Cause: Missing noise models or incorrect sensor placement Solution:

<sensor>
  <noise>
    <type>gaussian</type>
    <mean>0.0</mean>
    <stddev>0.01</stddev> <!-- Add realistic noise -->
  </noise>
</sensor>

Key Takeaways​

  • Gazebo provides high-fidelity physics simulation essential for robotics development
  • Multiple physics engines (ODE, Bullet, Simbody, DART) offer different trade-offs
  • SDF is Gazebo's native format; URDF is ROS-compatible and convertible
  • Accurate physics requires proper mass, inertia, friction, and contact parameters
  • Sensor simulation includes cameras, LiDAR, IMU, GPS, force/torque sensors
  • ROS 2 integration enables seamless testing of ROS-based control systems
  • Performance optimization involves balancing fidelity with computational cost
  • Headless and parallel simulations accelerate training for learning-based systems
