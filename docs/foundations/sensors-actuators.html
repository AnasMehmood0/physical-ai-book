02. Sensors and Actuators

The Physical Interface of Robotsâ€‹

Sensors and actuators form the critical interface between a robot's intelligence and the physical world. Sensors provide perceptionâ€”the ability to measure and understand the environmentâ€”while actuators enable actionâ€”the ability to move and manipulate objects.

Sensors: Perceiving the Worldâ€‹

Classification of Sensorsâ€‹

By Measurement Type:

  • Proprioceptive: Measure internal robot state (joint angles, motor current, battery level)
  • Exteroceptive: Measure external environment (distance, light, sound, force)

By Operating Principle:

  • Active: Emit energy and measure reflection (LiDAR, ultrasonic, radar)
  • Passive: Measure ambient energy (cameras, microphones, thermometers)

Vision Sensorsâ€‹

Camerasâ€‹

RGB Cameras:

  • Capture color images of the environment
  • High information density but requires significant processing
  • Affected by lighting conditions
  • Common resolutions: 640x480, 1280x720, 1920x1080
  • Applications: object recognition, visual servoing, human-robot interaction

Depth Cameras:

  • Measure distance to objects in the scene
  • Technologies:
    • Structured Light: Project pattern and triangulate (Intel RealSense, Kinect)
    • Time-of-Flight (ToF): Measure light travel time
    • Stereo Vision: Use two cameras to compute depth
  • Output: RGB + Depth (RGBD) images
  • Applications: 3D reconstruction, obstacle avoidance, manipulation

Event Cameras (DVS):

  • Record changes in brightness asynchronously
  • Very high temporal resolution (microseconds)
  • Low latency and power consumption
  • Applications: high-speed robotics, drone racing

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)â€‹

How it works:

  1. Emit laser pulses
  2. Measure time for reflection to return
  3. Calculate distance: distance = (speed of light Ã— time) / 2
  4. Rotate to scan 2D or 3D space

Types:

  • 2D LiDAR: Single scanning plane, ~270Â° coverage (Hokuyo, SICK)
  • 3D LiDAR: Multiple scanning planes or rotating head (Velodyne, Ouster, Livox)
  • Solid-State LiDAR: No moving parts, using MEMS or optical phased arrays

Characteristics:

  • Range: 10-200 meters depending on model
  • Accuracy: Â±2-5 cm
  • Update rate: 5-20 Hz
  • Weather resistant (better than cameras in fog/rain)

Applications:

  • Autonomous vehicles: obstacle detection, mapping
  • Mobile robots: navigation, SLAM
  • UAVs: terrain mapping, collision avoidance

Range Sensorsâ€‹

Ultrasonic Sensorsâ€‹

  • Emit high-frequency sound waves (40 kHz)
  • Measure time for echo to return
  • Range: 2 cm to 4 meters
  • Cheap and simple
  • Limited by beam width (cone-shaped measurement)
  • Affected by soft/absorbent surfaces
  • Applications: proximity detection, parking assistance

Infrared (IR) Distance Sensorsâ€‹

  • Emit infrared light and measure reflection
  • Types:
    • Sharp IR: Analog output proportional to distance
    • ToF IR: Digital distance measurement
  • Range: 10 cm to 1.5 meters typically
  • Fast response time
  • Affected by surface reflectivity and ambient light
  • Applications: edge detection, cliff avoidance, short-range obstacle detection

Inertial Measurement Units (IMU)â€‹

Components:

  • Accelerometer: Measures linear acceleration (3 axes)
  • Gyroscope: Measures angular velocity (3 axes)
  • Magnetometer: Measures magnetic field orientation (optional, 3 axes)

Output:

  • 6-DOF (accelerometer + gyroscope)
  • 9-DOF (add magnetometer)
  • Some include sensor fusion for orientation estimation (quaternion or Euler angles)

Characteristics:

  • High update rate: 100-1000 Hz
  • Drift over time (especially gyroscopes)
  • Used with filtering (Kalman, complementary)

Applications:

  • Orientation estimation
  • Motion tracking
  • Balancing robots
  • Dead reckoning
  • Vibration monitoring

Force and Tactile Sensorsâ€‹

Force/Torque Sensorsâ€‹

  • Measure forces and torques in 3D (6-axis)
  • Mounted at robot wrist or gripper
  • Used for force control and compliant manipulation
  • Precision: sub-Newton to hundreds of Newtons
  • Applications: assembly, grinding, human-robot collaboration

Tactile Sensorsâ€‹

  • Measure contact pressure distribution
  • Technologies:
    • Resistive: Pressure changes resistance
    • Capacitive: Pressure changes capacitance
    • Optical: Pressure deforms optical pattern
  • Arrays of sensing elements (taxels)
  • Applications: grasping, texture recognition, slip detection

GPS and GNSSâ€‹

Global Navigation Satellite Systems:

  • GPS (US), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (EU), BeiDou (China)
  • Outdoor positioning: Â±1-5 meters (consumer), Â±1 cm (RTK)
  • Requires clear sky view
  • Not suitable for indoor or dense urban environments
  • Applications: outdoor navigation, geo-referencing, surveying

RTK (Real-Time Kinematic):

  • Differential GPS with base station
  • Centimeter-level accuracy
  • Used in precision agriculture, surveying, UAVs

Actuators: Moving and Manipulatingâ€‹

Electric Motorsâ€‹

DC Motorsâ€‹

Brushed DC Motors:

  • Simple, cheap, easy to control
  • Speed proportional to voltage
  • Wear out due to brushes
  • Applications: hobby robots, low-cost systems

Brushless DC Motors (BLDC):

  • More efficient, longer lifespan
  • Require electronic speed controller (ESC)
  • Higher power density
  • Applications: drones, high-performance robots

Servo Motorsâ€‹

  • DC motor + position sensor (encoder/potentiometer) + control electronics
  • Closed-loop position control
  • Common in hobby robotics (PWM control)
  • Range: typically 180Â° (hobby servos)
  • Applications: robot arms, humanoids, walking robots

Stepper Motorsâ€‹

  • Rotate in discrete steps (e.g., 1.8Â° per step)
  • Open-loop position control (no encoder needed)
  • Can lose steps under high load
  • Good holding torque when energized
  • Applications: 3D printers, CNC machines, precise positioning

Dynamixel and Smart Servosâ€‹

  • High-performance servo motors with built-in controller
  • Serial communication (TTL, RS485, CAN)
  • Feedback: position, velocity, load, temperature
  • Programmable PID gains, limits, modes
  • Applications: research robots, humanoids (NAO, Pepper)

Hydraulic Actuatorsâ€‹

Advantages:

  • Very high force and power density
  • Smooth, continuous motion
  • Natural compliance (fluid compressibility)

Disadvantages:

  • Complex (pumps, valves, fluid management)
  • Maintenance intensive
  • Potential for leaks
  • Noisy

Applications:

  • Heavy machinery, construction equipment
  • Large humanoid robots (Atlas by Boston Dynamics)
  • Industrial robots requiring high force

Pneumatic Actuatorsâ€‹

Characteristics:

  • Use compressed air
  • Lightweight and fast
  • Inherently compliant and safe
  • Lower precision than electric or hydraulic
  • Require air compressor and regulators

Types:

  • Linear cylinders
  • Rotary actuators
  • Soft pneumatic actuators

Applications:

  • Pick-and-place operations
  • Soft robotics
  • Grippers
  • High-speed actuation

Linear Actuatorsâ€‹

Types:

  • Lead screw: Rotational to linear motion via threaded rod
  • Ball screw: Higher efficiency, lower friction
  • Belt drive: Fast, long stroke
  • Linear motor: Direct linear motion (no mechanical conversion)

Applications:

  • Gantry systems
  • Vertical lifts
  • Extendable arms

Grippers and End Effectorsâ€‹

Parallel Jaw Grippersâ€‹

  • Two fingers moving in parallel
  • Simple, effective for many objects
  • Limited adaptability

Multi-Fingered Handsâ€‹

  • Anthropomorphic or task-specific
  • Multiple degrees of freedom
  • Complex control
  • Examples: Shadow Hand, Allegro Hand, Robotiq hands

Suction Grippersâ€‹

  • Vacuum-based
  • Good for smooth, non-porous surfaces
  • Fast pick-and-place
  • Applications: warehouse automation, packaging

Soft Grippersâ€‹

  • Compliant materials (silicone, fabric)
  • Gentle grasping of delicate or irregular objects
  • Inherently adaptive
  • Applications: food handling, agriculture, human-safe interaction

Sensor Fusionâ€‹

Why Fuse Sensors?â€‹

Single sensors have limitations:

  • Noise and uncertainty
  • Limited field of view
  • Environmental dependencies
  • Failure modes

Combining multiple sensors provides:

  • Redundancy: Backup if one sensor fails
  • Complementarity: Different sensors measure different aspects
  • Increased accuracy: Fuse noisy measurements
  • Extended coverage: Combine fields of view

Common Fusion Approachesâ€‹

Kalman Filterâ€‹

  • Optimal linear estimator
  • Predicts state based on model
  • Updates prediction with sensor measurements
  • Balances model confidence vs. sensor confidence
  • Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) for nonlinear systems

Particle Filterâ€‹

  • Represents belief as set of weighted samples
  • Handles multimodal distributions
  • Nonlinear and non-Gaussian
  • Computationally expensive
  • Used in localization (Monte Carlo Localization)

Complementary Filterâ€‹

  • Simple, computationally light
  • High-pass filter on one sensor, low-pass on another
  • Common for IMU fusion (gyro for short-term, accelerometer for long-term)

Sensor Fusion Examplesâ€‹

IMU + GPS:

  • GPS provides absolute position (slow update, drift-free)
  • IMU provides high-rate motion (fast update, but drifts)
  • Fusion: accurate, high-rate position estimation

Camera + LiDAR:

  • Camera provides color and texture
  • LiDAR provides accurate depth
  • Fusion: colored point clouds, robust object detection

Wheel Odometry + IMU + Vision:

  • Wheel odometry estimates motion (subject to slip)
  • IMU detects orientation changes
  • Vision provides visual features for correction
  • Result: Visual-Inertial Odometry (VIO)

Selecting Sensors and Actuatorsâ€‹

Design Considerationsâ€‹

For Sensors:

  1. Measurement Range: What distances/forces/angles do you need to measure?
  2. Accuracy: How precise must measurements be?
  3. Update Rate: How fast must you sample?
  4. Environmental Conditions: Indoor/outdoor, lighting, weather
  5. Power Consumption: Battery-powered or wall-powered?
  6. Size and Weight: Constraints for mobile robots
  7. Cost: Budget per sensor and total system
  8. Interface: Analog, digital, communication protocol

For Actuators:

  1. Force/Torque Requirements: How much can it lift/move?
  2. Speed: How fast must it move?
  3. Precision: Positioning accuracy needed?
  4. Duty Cycle: Continuous or intermittent operation?
  5. Controllability: Open-loop or closed-loop?
  6. Safety: Compliance, force limiting, emergency stop
  7. Power Budget: Available power supply?
  8. Size/Weight: Mounting constraints?

Trade-offsâ€‹

  • High accuracy â†” High cost
  • Fast update rate â†” Higher power consumption
  • Large range â†” Lower precision
  • High force â†” Low speed (in actuators)
  • Simple & cheap â†” Limited capability

Interfacing with Hardwareâ€‹

Communication Protocolsâ€‹

Analog:

  • Voltage output (0-5V, 0-10V)
  • Current output (4-20mA)
  • Simple but susceptible to noise

Digital:

  • I2C: Serial, multi-device, short distance
  • SPI: Serial, fast, short distance
  • UART/Serial: Point-to-point, common
  • CAN: Robust, automotive standard, moderate speed
  • Ethernet: High-speed, long distance, complex
  • USB: Widely supported, moderate speed
  • Wireless: WiFi, Bluetooth, Zigbee for untethered operation

Signal Conditioningâ€‹

  • Amplification: Boost weak signals
  • Filtering: Remove noise (low-pass, high-pass, notch)
  • Calibration: Map raw values to physical units
  • Linearization: Correct for non-linear sensor responses

Key Takeawaysâ€‹

  • Sensors provide perception; actuators enable action
  • Different sensor types for different modalities: vision (cameras, LiDAR), range (ultrasonic, IR), motion (IMU), force (tactile, F/T)
  • Actuators vary by technology: electric (DC, servo, stepper), hydraulic, pneumatic
  • No single sensor is perfect; sensor fusion combines strengths
  • Selection depends on application requirements: range, accuracy, speed, cost, environment
  • Proper interfacing requires understanding communication protocols and signal conditioning
  • The physical layer is critical: even the best AI can't overcome poor sensing or actuation
